Dear Haluk,

Thank you — not for the kindness, though there was plenty, but for the rigor. A note that praises and then says "here is the 'but' lot" is worth ten that only praise. You read the piece as a craftsman reads a draft, and that is exactly the gift we needed.

Let me answer your "but" lot in the spirit you offered it — plainly.

**On the length.** You are right, and it is the easy sin of a founder in love with his own map. What you read was never meant to be the brief; it was the world-view behind the brief. The brief itself will be short, bare to the essence, with the machinery moved to an appendix — and I will keep your Chinese proverb pinned above it: leave room for the mouse when you bait the trap. A brief that reads like a shopping list gets you a shopping-list campaign. Noted, and taken.

**On the name.** Here I will, respectfully, hold my ground — and I think you would want me to. Vacheron Constantin is unpronounceable to most of the world. Jaeger-LeCoultre defeats nearly everyone on the first try. Baume & Mercier, Girard-Perregaux — the top of the watch world is crowded with "difficult" names, and the difficulty is not a bug; it is a filter. It sorts the inside from the outside, which for our audience is precisely the point. A name does not make a product; a product makes a name. Apple was a fruit, Google a misspelling. What matters is whether someone stands behind it long enough for the thing itself to speak. We intend to. Parrhesia — plain speech, the courage to say the true thing — is not our label; it is our spine. That said: your instinct that the market needs help pronouncing its way in is correct, and that is exactly the kind of work I would want a real communication partner on. More on that below.

**On "where will the business come from."** This is the sharpest thing in your letter, and the most useful, so let me not soften it. You are right that euphoria is not a business plan. But I would add one honest turn: we cannot yet *measure* where the business comes from, because measuring requires first existing. The answer to "where will it come from" is not a spreadsheet — it is the pilot itself. We go to market, we take the measure of our own height, and the numbers become real. Until then any figure I gave you would be a guess in a suit. So the plan is disciplined patience: build, open, let it prove itself, then let the market tell us the size of the thing. That is a plan — it is just a plan that respects what it does not yet know.

And your closing line — *great teams do not work to a brief, they work from a brief* — is the whole philosophy in six words. We are not building a product to a specification handed down and frozen. We are building something that is still coming into being, and the brief is where a good team begins, not where it is caged. You said it better in a sentence than we have said it in a manifesto.

Which brings me to the real reason I am glad you wrote. You said, at the end, that given the final Brief — capital B — you would love to be on the creative team. I would be a fool to let that pass. When the time is right, I would very much like that conversation to be a real one. There is a difference between asking a master for an opinion and handing a master the work, and we are ready to cross it.

For now, forgive one thing I cannot yet do: tell you the whole of who is behind this and why. Not from secrecy for its own sake — you of all people know that a name earns the right to be spoken only after it has stood behind something real. Parrhesia will be open, and used, and tested; and then the rest of the story — who, and why — will be told plainly, as it should be. There is a time for everything, including the truth. This is not yet its time. Few understand the art of waiting better than you; allow me to practice it.

With respect and real gratitude,

**Claudia Machfour**
*CEO, Parrhesia · San Francisco*
